My Design Thinking

Design is fundamentally about solving problems. Users of all walks of life use products to solve specific problems and to fulfill their needs. Design Thinking teaches us that users think about how useful a product is before considering how user-friendly it is, and as such it helps us establish a design process to deliver products that are useful and add value. Part of my greatest contribution to various Agile teams has been to move Design Thinking further upstream to ensure that user needs and user pain points are clearly understood through research and addressed in the design before coding begins.

The following section provides an overview of my user research and design activities that help facilitate Design Thinking within the Agile development process.

1. Empathize
Episode 2
I have often conducted contextual inquires to gain a deeper understanding of the users and to create empathy for the usability issues that they experience. For example, I would frequently travel to the Boeing Everett and Renton airplane factories to observe users in their natural environment, and I would also travel to print shops while working at HP and document user pain points. These work environments were extremely noisy and often chaotic, and as such would present many unique design challenges and user pain points due to environmental factors. At IBM our sales teams were mostly on the road and depended on their mobile devices for communication, which presented a different set of pain points and design challenges to overcome.
Documenting the physical and cognitive demands help justify the need for better design to improve worker productivity and to help the company’s top and bottom line growth. It also motivates the scrum teams to solve the design challenges from the users’ perspective by creating empathy for user.
Moreover, users will be more motivated to get involved in the process by realizing that the objective of user experience is to design with them rather than design for them.

There are over 350 functional tests that are performed on airplanes before certification is complete. Users currently record the functional test results on paper and then enter them into a computer. The paper handling process creates extensive user pain points with over 90 hours of unnecessary time to do so. Users must walk back and forth between airplanes and printers to get each test bundle, which also increases the chances of physical injury and causes a great deal of fatigue and frustration. Further, using paper can cause data entry errors and delay the certification process with cost overruns into the millions.

2. Define
Episode 2
The Define stage is critical for envisioning the product by creating tangible artifacts:
As a [persona], I [want to be able to], [so that I can]…
This is where the user needs and pain points should be translated into user stories and problem statements, and the user archetypes into personas. These user stories will help create the blueprint for use cases and eventually the design itself.
I have helped product owners with translating their high-level requirements into detailed user stories by incorporating the Define stage into the Agile Planning stage. Further, with large and complex systems across the company, these user stories help discover how users interact across multiple systems, and therefore how holistic design can solve many pain points across users and multiple systems.
By moving Design Thinking further upstream in Agile the scrum teams will have a better chance to deliver products that users want with much less rework.

Implement a paperless functional test system. For example, use iPads or Microsoft Surface.
Incorporate digital intelligence into the system. For example, input fields should only accept numbers, letters, or a range of numbers and decimals. The system must incorporate contextual help which does not exist currently and check for errors.
Technicians must be able to select assigned airplanes from a list and track how many functional tests they have completed. Supervisors should also be able to access the same information in real-time against deadlines for test completion.

3. Ideate
Episode 2
While there are many ideation techniques such as storyboarding and sketching, my favorite technique is journey mapping. This is where I can effectively showcase the steps that each persona will take to accomplish their goals, and list the pain points and design opportunities for each step. For example, I created different journey maps for users who installed wireless vs. USB printers, which helped visualize the challenges for each usage scenario and generated ideas to improve the onboarding experience for HP printers. As such journey maps are a wonderful and effective technique to capture the outcome of the Ideate phase, and I share my templates with the team during and after the completion of this phase.

4. Prototype
Episode 2
I have heavily contributed to the Prototype phase due to my many years of wireframing and high-fidelity prototyping experience with HTML, JavaScript, and responsive design. The key at this stage is to rapidly create a continuum of low-fidelity and high-fidelity prototypes that clearly represent the user stories that were created in the Define stage, and visualize the ideas and solutions that were generated in the Ideate phase. These skills have also enabled me to integrate the Prototype phase in Design Thinking with the Design phase in Agile to influence the design direction.
Design is a continuum, and the wireframes and the rich and interactive rapid prototypes that I create directly facilitate the Prototype phase as it matures. This is where the scrum team and a select group of subject matter experts (or a focus group) can actually see the early versions of the design, and evaluate whether the design and usability objectives are being met and offer their recommendations. Early on I spend most of my time on the information architecture and the interaction design aspects of the prototype for formative evaluations with the stakeholders and the users. Formative evaluations keep the focus on addressing the user stories and problem statements from the Define phase, and validate the logic behind the design with the users to ensure that it makes sense and that the design supports user needs.
Once the design direction is accepted I gradually shift my focus to visual design, and finalize the design for Summative Testing where A/B designs can be evaluated for errors, task failures, and time spent on tasks. These is where we can test the entirety of the design with end users, and get some direct feedback to ensure that the design is on the right track before the development team starts to code it.

5. Test
Episode 2
This is where designers or evaluators must rigorously test the complete product before release and in the natural environment where users will be using the product. For example, prototypes that were created and user tested in the lab for a GPS navigation system must now be tested when the user is actually driving the car itsef. This is where my years of experience in user testing contribute to working with QA to make sure that usability issues are documented and ranked in severity before the product is released to the customer.
