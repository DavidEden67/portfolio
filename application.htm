Web and Desktop Applications

Designing thin client and thick client applications has been a central part of my career. Early in my career I started designing thick client applications for Microsoft Windows. I was a Visual Basic programmer for several years which tremendously helped me with designing rich user interfaces quickly and effectively for operating systems. As the internet evolved, I used my prior Visual Basic experience to adapt to designing client-server applications in HTML and web scripting languages, which ultimately paved the way for designing mobile and responsive applications in the future. These applications were designed for the mass consumer markets as well as for internal corporate use.

Given the fact that product design is a continuum, my prototyping skills help evolve the design from simple wireframe concepts to highly interactive designs that represent the information architecture, the interaction design, onscreen animations, and the visual design.

My interactive prototypes have been critically important for demonstrating design concepts to company executives and product owners; for conducing usability inspections and usability tests, and for communicating with product development teams. They have been used for lab and remote user testing, and have saved unnecessary costs by discovering usability issues and missing user requirements early in  the design process.

The following case studies serve as a few examples of my design contributions in various industries that are non-proprietary and I can share with you. I have also captured videos to demonstrate the types of high-fidelity interactive prototypes that I create for complex user interactions.

Customer Support Applications

Product design and product support often go hand in hand to create a superior user experience. As part of my responsibilities at Dell, HP, and Iomega, I was asked to help redesign customer support sites to help users with a seamless hardware and software installation experience. I also had to propose better methods for troubleshooting to cut down on product support calls that could wipe out any profit on the sale of our consumer products.

One of the most recent design challenges that I faced at HP was that our customers often relied on YouTube videos and Google search to install or troubleshoot printers. Many of these videos available on the internet are not created by qualified technicians and often contain major errors which negatively impact the user experience, and ultimately lead to costly customer support calls and a lower NPS score for products.

The design challenge therefore was to direct users to our product support page to guide the users through proper installation, troubleshooting, and provide learning material all in one place. As such we created a new user journey to address the aforementioned challenges in 3 simple steps.

  • Upon unboxing the printer, the Quickstart Guide was simplified to direct the user to the product installation page for a specific printer. Instead of complicated and often confusing illustrations on paper, the user could view a brief video of the product installation, and then navigate through the guided installation steps to install the hardware. Through research we had discovered that installation videos were highly desired by customers, which is why they would often rely on YouTube even if the information was incorrect, and the design addressed this pain point.
  •  After hardware setup was complete, the user would be guided to put the machine on the home Wi-Fi network.
  •  The guided installation would then instruct the user to download the correct app to automatically detect the printer model and install it on any device to print, scan, and fax.

This approach was significantly more user-friendly and effective since it helped prevent users from skipping important installation steps and from installing incorrect software, which was not often the case with traditional printed instructions and illustrations that were long and confusing. It also simplified designing for international audience due to ease of translation and communicating with users through animations and videos .

The following two videos demonstrate the mobile and the desktop versions of my design which I created in HTML. Careful effort was spent on the mobile version since most users preferred to carry a phone with them and refer to the steps while installing their printers.

Complex Mobile Apps

One of the mot significant design challenges that I have faced in recent years is designing for the mobile environment. Small devices are a very taxing design environment since every pixel counts, and are more error prone than the traditional mouse and keyboard. As such I have had to spend many more hours on being creative and working within the limitations of touch-enabled devices, and spending more time to validate those designs with stakeholders and the end users than desktop applications.

For example, while at Boeing, I designed the company's first mobile application for airplane certification. Electronic Just in Time Testing empowered airplane technicians to conduct hundreds of functional tests on airplanes and record the test results directly on tablets (rather than writing test results first on paper and then recording them on a PC, thus saving 90 hours of labor on average per airplane). Test results would be immediately sent from tablets to a computer and analyzed in real time, which saved the company millions of dollars to immediatley discover and mitigate certification issues.

While at IBM, I also designed the first app for the sales teams that would immediately alert them to new sales opportunities in various regions, and helped them bid on contracts as soon as they went public. Such an app enhanced the sales teams' situational awareness that positively impacted the top line by generating millions of dollars in revenue.

Analytical and Operational Dashboards

One of the most difficult design challenges that I tackled while working at Boeing and IBM were projects that involved analytical and operational dashboards. Some were complex in-house applications that required the creation and management of content; approval of tasks in a workflow, and various levels of user access rights. I also had to closely simulate the Tableau user interface and design within its limitations for simpler projects that were designed entirely in Tableau.

Much of my success in dashboard design projects depended on my ability to show how data was presented and manipulated. HTML provides great flexibility to show data tables; allow users to input data and select from a set of data, and then be presented with charts and other relevant information. Here I could contribute to the full stack by working with back-end and front-end developers to demonstrate how every element of the design functioned.

For example, while working at Boeing, Flight Test Operations required real-time operational dashboards to measure actual performance versus targets. Executives required analytical dashboards to understand what worked and what did not historically. For example:

  • We needed to gather data in real time to measure how many flight test plans had changed within 24 hours of testing, given the fact that the ideal target was zero.
  • We had to display how many test airplanes were landing late vs. the planned arrival.
  • After the tests, we had to display the analytical data in a way to historically understand where the operational problems had occurred, and why.

Dell IT Assistant (ITA)

Dell ITA was a highly ambitious project which required broad and direct leadership for the full UCD cycle. The company executives were looking for solutions to the following problems:

How can we control Dell devices and applications from a centralized console? How can we obtain detailed hardware and software inventory information from a centralized database and identify issues as they happen? How can system administrators create and run scheduled tasks for selected devices and establish dynamic groups of devices? How can we create a role-based and customizable user interface for system administrators?

The solution would reduce downtime significantly and and vastly improve system admin productivity. I was tasked with conducting user research initially. System Administrators were interviewed and their goals were documented in detail. Following the Allen Cooper model, I documented what users wanted to see in the user interface and how it should be organized (the information architecture), and what they wanted to do with the interface (the interaction design model).

Documenting user tasks led me to rely on some of the familiar design metaphors in the Microsoft Windows Human Interface Guidelines, and to take advantage of what the users were already familiar with. The System Administrators could potentially manage hundreds of devices, and the devices had a parent-child nesting relationship and needed bulk actions on a group of devices or direct actions on an individual device. I decided to use a Treeview (aka Windows Explorer) control to design the nesting relationship of the devices; tabs for viewing distinct categories of information for each device, and a Listview control to display data in columns. The interface would be highly modular and customizable by the user, and could grow and adapt as more features were added to it in the future. It could also adapt to the user roles and needs: system admins could be assigned any number of devices depending on their user privileges and only monitor those devices. I created my final interactive prototypes in Visual Studio for user testing and for demos to stakeholders and software developers.

The design met all the 5 objectives of usability: Useful, Learnable, Efficient, Forgiving, and Satisfaying . It has been used to manage millions of Dell servers and other devices since its inception, and is still being perfected by Dell based on my original design concept. This is one of my design contributions that I am most proud of.

FiatLux 3D

3D Medical Imaging is one of the most interesting and challenging projects that I have worked on. Most radiologists have been trained to view images in the 2D environment. 3D medical imaging offers the enormous potential for fly through user experience where a physician can actually view the reconstructed patient images immersively (very similar to Augmented Reality). One of FiatLux's main advantages was that it enabled physicians to actually carry a tablet with them and view the patient files on the go. To be successful however we had to address several design problems based on user research:

How can we create a mobile experience for medical imaging, and empower physicians to view patient files anywhere? How can we present medical images to physicians based on their needs and specialties? For example, how can we filter out medical images for Orthopedic surgeons, Splanchnology, and Neurosurgeons? How can we also ensure that the device can be used for traditional radiology in the 2D environment as well as in 3D?

The solution was to present the medical imaging in both 2D and in 3D based on user needs. For example, in traditional radiology the physician is mostly  interested in viewing a fracture from Front, Back, Left, and Right as shown in my prototype below. He or she could then switch to 3D to get a better sense of a compound fracture for the same patient. A heart surgeon would be able to filter out all other images such as skeletal and view the cardiovascular reconstruction only. A neurosurgeon could view a tumor and print a 3D model of it on a 3D printer.

As such FiatLux offered an attractive alternative to physicians to view and study medical images without being tied to traditional workstations.

HP Embedded Server

The Embedded Web Server was the most ambitious usability project that I was assigned to at HP. The company executives and project managers were looking for solutions to solve the following problems:

How can we empower our customers to control network laser printers remotely through a browser? How can we alert our laser printer customers that they need to buy supplies and help them automate the ordering process, and as such increase the revenues on printer supplies? How can printers run self-diagnostics and alert the system admins about potential issues? How can HP product support connect to a printer remotely and detect device issues to save costs on technical support?

The answer to many of these usability issues was the Embedded Web Server, a revolutionary product that paved the path for using a web browser to access the firmware on a peripheral device and interact with it remotely. This invention also made it far more convenient for the end user to monitor and configure printers on a computer screen rather than using the small and cumbersome control panels on the physical device itself.

I worked closely with the marketing department to document both user and business requirements. I identified the personas (office users and system admins) and their usage patterns, and designed a role-based user interface that would show or hide options based on user privileges. I created the high-fidelity prototype shown below for user testing and for final handoff to developers. The end result was a quantum leap in ease of use that helped grow both the top line and the bottom line for the company.

Boeing GoldCare

Boeing GoldCare is one of the most complex and rewarding projects in my career. It challenged me through the entire UCD cycle for a revolutionary product that had never existed before. GoldCare came about with the birth of the Boeing 787 which is revolutionizing the air travel. The 787 does not just provide more fuel efficiency or ease of manufacturing: the 787 also has enabling technologies that can send out real data about the health of the airplane and its critical parts. Before the 787, Maintenance Repair Organizations around the world had to deal with many unexpected issues that would keep airplanes out of the air and in the hangers for repair. Many airlines were plagued with the following problems that GoldCare aimed to solve:

If an airplane experiences a critical issue in flight and needs to be diverted for emergency repairs, can we identify the problem in advance and estimate how long it will take to fix the problem? What replacement parts will be needed or do we need to fly the parts into an airport? What tools will be required to replace the part, and will the airport have them? Will the airport have the skilled technicians to replace the parts or do we have to fly them in? How long reasonably will it take to repair the airplane, and will the passengers have to deplane and will the airline need to provide another flight?

Boeing GoldCare project was a quantum leap in helping solve those problems. Through the enabling technologies of the 787, parts like engines can send data on their health and the severity of the issue and immediately alert the airlines. As such we could create a system to monitor the health of the aircraft and take action to keep them flying on time.

My role in the project was pretty challenging. I needed to create a visual design to display where the airplanes were at any given time around the world, which airlines they belonged to, and if there was a problem, what distress signals were being transmitted by the airplane and how to make the data understandable to the repair organizations. I also had to help design an intelligent system to help the operators locate where the nearest parts were located and where to find the qualified technicians to repair the airplane if they were not available at the repair site.

All this effort largely succeeded due to interviewing the end users regarding their goals and understanding their pain points with the existing workflows, and by involving them throughout the design cycle by using participatory design.

The project is now being expanded to a new generation of Boeing airplanes such as the 777 that have enabling technologies as well.
