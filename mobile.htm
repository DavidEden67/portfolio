How I design for touchscreens

One of the most significant challenges with touch interaction design is that users hold and interact with mobile devices differently. User's physical characteristics also play a major factor. I adhere to the following rules when designing for mobile and touchscreen devices:

  • Controls must be 7-10mm so that they can be tapped accurately.
  • Visual Design must clearly show contrast among controls and the screen background without requiring too much user concentration.
  • Interaction Design must clearly provide feedback when an object as been tapped and if the user needs to wait for the action to be completed.
  • Text must be easy to read without having to pinch and zoom.
  • Device orientation must be treated differently since text and other objects can appear smaller in portrait view.
  • Design for hand position in a way that the most frequently used controls are within the reach of the thumb, and that the user can scroll with the thumb without tapping on unintended objects.
  • Minimize the need for typing by creating shortcuts or by storing frequently used preferences.

These are some of the main reasons that lead me to create and user test a prototype on the mobile device itself even if the lifelike PC simulations meet all the design objectives.

IBM Sales Team App

The first app that I designed at IBM was for the IBM Sales Team. The sales force consisted of true road warriors who engaged the customers and impacted the company bottom line more than any other. UX owed it to them to help create an app to give them an edge over the competition. My mission was to create a focus group of the people who actually worked in the trenches; understand their goals and pain points, and build an app on top of it.  The sales team members (no matter what company they work for) seem to have the following common goals and pain points:

How can I get immediately alerted when there is an opportunity in my area of interest, such as  an announcement by a company that they need to purchase 10 million dollars worth of servers? If I decide to pursue that opportunity, how will it change my annual sales target and will I be able to reach or exceed it? How will it change my commission? How can I see not only how I am doing individually but my entire team in terms of the annual sales targets?

Initial user research revealed that app users did not want to see and do anything and everything on their mobile devices. For example, they wanted to use the desktop version of the app for more complex tasks such as creating spreadsheets. The sales team wanted the mobile app to create awareness and alert them to critical information while on the road.

As such the 80/20 rule very much applied to the information architecture of the app: the small screen had to give clear priority to the 20% of the features that were used over 80% of the time. Further, I had to figure out how to start from the high level visual information and progressively narrow it down to show more details. Bootstrap actually provided me with what I needed. The Accordion Panel and the Carousel widget helped me include more details for each visual piece of information without cluttering up the screen.

Boeing Electronic Just In Time Testing App (eJITT)

eJITT was the most impactful mobile application that was designed by User Experience at Boeing Commercial, and I was very proud to be a part of it. By applying user research and Design Thinking the problem statement boiled down to the following.

Problem Statement:

There are over 400 functional tests that must be performed on each airplane by Quality Assurance and Shop (technicians) separately. All the functional tests are currently done using paper records, causing significant effort and delay to print and record the results of functional tests by each persona and then compare the results manually to certify an airplane.

Purposed Solution:

  • Each functional test must be recorded electronically on a mobile tablet device which can be carried around the airplane without any need for paper records.
  • By electronically comparing the test results from QA and Shop, a significant amount of time will be saved over comparing the test results manually thus streamlining the user workflow.
  • The electronic system must be intelligent. For example, certain fields must only accept numeric values or certain number of decimal points.
  • System must intelligently alert the user to data entry errors.
  • Users must be able to access online help for data entry which is currently lacking.
  • System must alert the user to which tests have not been completed (e.g., number of tests not completed or percent completion).
The mobile app that I designed not only vastly streamlined the user workflow and reduced cognitive load, but it also contributed to the bottom line by saving airplane build by about 94 hours per airplane. The usability of the design itself benefited from much of the mobile design principles that I discussed earlier in this section.

Boeing Flight Test App

I have already covered Boeing Fight Test under the Application Design section in my portfolio. However, one of the best rewards of working on this project was the creation of my first mobile application: a highly useful app that allowed our user base to track when the test airplanes were out of the gate, off the ground, on the ground, and in the gate. Further, users could be alerted if there was an emergency situation with any of the airplanes and get involved right away. Previously our users were glued to their computers to watch the flight status of the airplanes and detect if there was an emergency situation with them. With the mobile app the data could be directly transmitted from the Operations Center to the mobile app, freeing our users from having to monitor test airplanes constantly and as such they could use their time to do far more productive tasks while the app monitored the airplane status for them.
