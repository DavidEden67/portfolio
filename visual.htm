Visual Design

While I have spent years on perfecting my Interaction Design skills, I have also spent a great deal of time on learning Visual Design and applying its principles to my work. Users initially form ideas about the functionality of the user interface from static elements before they actually click or tap on the screen itself. Further, user research has consistently demonstrated that aesthetic designs are perceived as being more user-friendly and satisfying to use. CSS3 has opened up new vistas for visual design, and it is essential for a UX designer to understand CSS3 to be able to deliver clean and pixel perfect visual designs that attract users.

Visual design components

As a UX designer I have had to understand and follow established style guides at major companies. However, there have been many occasions when I was asked to create or suggest new visual components from simple buttons to more complex interactive tabs and accordion panels. For certain projects I also had to create a style guide from the ground up by defining the tokens and components, and then hand off the CSS3 files to developers in order to create a consistent design language for various projects.

Gamification techniques in recent years have also created great success for certain apps such as TikTok, which help engage users and make them feel that they enjoy interacting with the user interface. As such I have had to me mindful of the latest visual design trends in addition to my traditional responsibilities for user research and interaction design. 

I have included some examples of how I create tokens and components. These examples demonstrate the use of static elements for typography, colors, iconography, as well as the CSS3 selectors for hover, active, and focus which must blend successfully to create the user experience for modern visual design. The components are modular and can be used to build an interactive user interface rapidly.

How I incorporate animations and motion in visual design

Animations are playing a major role in the new attention economy: apps and websites increasingly revolve around the human attention span and using gamification to communicate with users. With the end of support for motion design in Flash, CSS3 and Vector Graphics are filling the gap for visual designers. When used properly, animations play a vital role in capturing users' attention and helping them accomplish their tasks more successfully. As such it is critical to incorporate animations when design is being transformed from simple wireframes into more advanced prototypes and evaluate their effectiveness with users.

Here I have created a demonstration of some of my routine visual design transition techniques for fade, zoom, rotation, and motion that are eye-catching yet without being overwhelming. Such animations have been especially effective in communicating alerts and other critical information to users (especially in the mobile environment where users are more distracted).
